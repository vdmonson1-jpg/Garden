# Rosemary

> *Salvia rosmarinus* (formerly *Rosmarinus officinalis*) | Lamiaceae

## Overview

Rosemary is a woody perennial Mediterranean herb with needle-like leaves and a piney, resinous aroma. Drought-tolerant and long-lived, it can grow into a large shrub over time. It is an excellent container plant and pairs beautifully with roasted meats, potatoes, and bread. Nearly maintenance-free once established.

## Growing Conditions

| Factor           | Details                        |
|------------------|--------------------------------|
| Sun              | Full sun (6–8+ hours)          |
| Water            | Low; drought tolerant once established |
| Soil pH          | 6.0–7.0                        |
| Soil Type        | Sandy, well-draining; tolerates poor soil |
| Spacing          | 18–24 inches apart             |
| Hardiness Zone   | Zones 7–11 (perennial); zones 4–6 (annual or overwinter indoors) |
| Days to Maturity | Harvest lightly in year 1; full production year 2+ |

## Planting

Start from cuttings or transplants — seed germination is slow and unreliable. Plant in spring after last frost. Ensure excellent drainage; rosemary will rot in wet, heavy soil. Raised beds and sandy mixes work well.

## Care

### Watering
Water deeply but infrequently. Allow soil to dry between waterings. Established plants are very drought tolerant. Do not overwater — this is the #1 killer.

### Feeding
Very light feeder. Too much fertilizer produces lush but flavorless growth. A light compost top-dress in spring is sufficient.

### Pruning & Training
Prune after flowering to keep compact and bushy. Do not cut into old woody growth — it rarely regrows from bare wood. Harvest tips regularly to encourage branching.

### Pest & Disease
Generally pest resistant. Watch for powdery mildew in humid conditions, spider mites in dry indoor conditions, and root rot from poor drainage.

## Companion Planting

| Good Companions              | Bad Companions |

| Beans, Cabbage, Carrots      | Basil          |

## Harvest

Snip stem tips 2–3 inches long at any time. Harvest before flowering for peak flavor. Strip needles from woody stems for cooking.

## Varieties to Try

- **Tuscan Blue** — Upright, vigorous; excellent for cooking
- **Prostratus** — Trailing/creeping form; ornamental; good for pots and walls
- **Arp** — Cold-hardy; best for zone 6 and borderline regions
