# Bougainvillea

> *Bougainvillea* spp. | Nyctaginaceae

## Overview

Bougainvillea is a stunning tropical and subtropical vine known for its vivid papery bracts — the showy "flowers" — in shades of magenta, red, orange, pink, white, and purple. The actual flowers are tiny white tubes; the color comes from the surrounding bracts. Vigorous and sun-loving, it's perfect for covering trellises, walls, and fences. Thorny stems make it an effective barrier as well.

## Growing Conditions

| Factor           | Details                        |
|------------------|--------------------------------|
| Sun              | Full sun (5+ hours minimum; more = more blooms) |
| Water            | Moderate; drought tolerant once established |
| Soil pH          | 5.5–6.5                        |
| Soil Type        | Well-draining; tolerates poor soil |
| Spacing          | 3–10 feet depending on use     |
| Hardiness Zone   | Perennial zones 9–11; container/annual in colder zones |
| Days to Maturity | Blooms within first season if well-established |

## Planting

Best planted in spring. Use cuttings or nursery transplants. Disturb roots as little as possible at planting — bougainvillea is sensitive to transplant shock. Grows vigorously in containers, which can actually encourage more blooming by slightly stressing the plant.

## Care

### Watering
Water regularly until established, then reduce. Slight drought stress between waterings triggers blooming. Overwatering produces lush green growth at the expense of bracts. In containers: water, let dry slightly, repeat.

### Feeding
Fertilize with a bloom-boosting fertilizer (higher phosphorus/potassium) during growing season. Avoid high-nitrogen feeds which push vegetative growth. Monthly feeding in spring/summer is typical.

### Pruning & Training
Prune after each bloom cycle to encourage the next flush. Train onto trellis or support with ties — it does not self-attach like true climbers. Wear gloves; thorns are sharp. Hard pruning in late winter rejuvenates overgrown plants.

### Pest & Disease
Aphids, caterpillars, scale, leaf miners. Generally quite tough. Root rot from overwatering is the biggest threat. Ensure excellent drainage.

## Harvest

Not a food plant. "Harvest" bracts for cut flower arrangements — they hold their color well when dried.

## Varieties to Try

- **Barbara Karst** — Deep red/magenta; one of the most vigorous
- **California Gold** — Golden yellow bracts; striking
- **Raspberry Ice** — Variegated foliage; magenta bracts; compact
